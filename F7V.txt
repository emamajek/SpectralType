F7V

Garrison94 does not list an F7V anchor standard, and MK73 does not
list an MK73 dagger standard.

None of the standards is particularly great, at least in terms of
representing a near solar-metallicity F7V that is close to the
color-mag main sequence. tet Per is worthy of some follow-up to see if
it is truly halfway between F6V and F8V -- but its [Fe/H], Mv, and B-V
are suggestive that it might be very representative of ~solar
metallicity F7V stars (i.e. might be the best candidate standard of
the bunch). iot Psc (= HD 222368) probably has the best pedigree of
the F7V standards, however it appears to be slightly metal poor.

Houk/Michigan classifications: Houk's Michigan Spectral Survey
catalogued 2370 stars as "F7V". Of these, 42 were later classified by
Gray and collaborators (2001, 2003, 2006) with the median subtype
being F8V (clipped mean F7.6 +-0.2 s.e.m., +-1.2 st.dev.).  About a
third of the Houk F8V stars were classified as stars of later hydrogen
type with metal poor flags by Gray et al. (e.g. F8 through G0 with Fe
and CH flags; the latest being "G0V Fe-0.8 CH-0.5"). I recommend
grouping Houk's F7V stars with F7.5Vs classified since the 1970s
(e.g. Gray's). Note that the median Teff of stars classified by Houk
as F7V in the PASTEL compilation of Teffs (530 values) is 6208 K
(+-168 K 1sigma scatter).

# The "Kraft Break" and Hot/Cool Star Boundary

Several authors adopt the Teff boundary ("Kraft Break") separating
"hot stars" (fast-rotating, thinning convective zones) and "cool
stars" (rotation slows with age, deeper convective zones) around Teff
~ 6250K, which is closest to the F7V subtype on our adopted Teff scale
(appropriate for ~solar-ish composition main sequence stars).

Kraft65 finds the change in rotation behavior among the Hyades to be
around Mv=3.6 and (B-V)=0.43, for which the median vsini = 40 km/s
(noting that <Vsini> trended 80 km/s at B-V=0.35 (Mv=3.0) down to 15
km/s at B-V=0.493 (Mv=4.0). For ~solar stars B-V=0.43 is ~F5V, and Mv
= 3.6 corresponds to ~F6V. Bohm-Vitense02 does a deeper study of the
change in behavior of chromospheric and transition layer emission line
fluxes among F-type Hyads, and notes "We found that at the spectral
type F5 most chromospheric lines show a steep decrease of emission
line fluxes for B-V between 0.42 and 0.45, parallel to the decrease of
the rotational velocities."

Reiners03 shows (Fig.5) that among a sample of field F/G stars, stars
with vsini < 10 km/s appear to be rare among stars hotter than
B-V=0.40 (~F3.5V) and stars with vsini > 10 km/s appear to be rare
among stars cooler than B=V=0.60 (~G0V). Reiners03 Fig.12 shows that
the transition appears to be focussed on roughly Teff ~6000K to
~6400K. At Teff=6000K, vsini > 12 km/s stars appear only rarely near
the ZAMS (presumably young) and then among evolved ~1.5-1.7 Msun stars
leaving the MS. Hotward of Teff=6400K, nearly all stars have vsini >
12 km/s.  At Teff ~ 6250K, once stars finding vsini>12 km/s stars
among the >1.4 Msun stars in the 2nd half of their MS lifetimes and
~1.3-1.4 Msun stars in the 1st half of their MS lifetime. So Fig. 12
of Reiners03 seems to support Teff ~ 6250K being a reasonable place to
put the "Kraft Break", recognizing that the transition appears to
happen about +-200K of this Teff.

Bieryla15, Temple18, Martinez20, Stevens20, Santos21, Vanderberg22,
Santos23 also quote the Kraft Break as being near Teff=6250K. Although
Kraft67 is often cited, Kraft himself does not list a Teff in that
paper. 

Avallone22 provides a concise modern description of the boundary: "The
Kraft break is a transition that is seen across stellar effective
temperatures (Teff). Hot stars (Teff > 6250 K) typically experience
little to no angular momentum loss due to the presence of vanishingly
thin convective zones that cannot sufficiently support a magnetized
wind. Cool stars (Teff < 6250 K) typically experience substantial
angular momentum loss due to the presence of large convection zones
that can sustain a magnetized wind (Kraft 1967)."

Psaridi23 quotes the Kraft Break as being "Teff >~ 6200K, ~<F8V or M*
>~ 1.1 Msun".

Host stars of misaligned Jupiters appear at Teff > 6250K (Winn10,
Dawson14, Albrecht22). Albrecht22: "The narrowing of the obliquity
distribution of HJ [Hot Jupiter] hosts as the effective temperature
becomes lower than about 6250 K (Section 3.1.5). The transition
temperature appears to be coincident with the “Kraft break,” below
which cool stars have deep convective envelopes and undergo magnetic
braking."

Beyer & White (2023; https://arxiv.org/pdf/2408.02638) survey the
nearby F dwarfs within 33.33pc: "Nearly all stars redder than GBP −
GRP = 0.60 mag are slowly rotating (vsini ≲ 20 km/s), while only 4 of
40 stars bluer than GBP − GRP = 0.54 mag are slowly rotating,
consistent with that expected for a random distribution of
inclinations. The Break is centered at an effective temperature of
6550 K and has a width of about 200 K, corresponding to a mass range
of 1.32−1.41 M⊙. The Break is ∼ 450 K hotter than the stellar
temperature at which hot Jupiters show a change in their obliquity
distribution, often attributed to tidal realignment." "The above
analysis shows that the Kraft Break is well-defined, occurring at a
central effective temperature of ∼ 6550 K, central GBP − GRP color of
∼ 0.57 mag, and central mass of 1.37 M⊙. The Break is relatively
sharp, having a width of only 200 K in temperature, ∼ 0.06 mag in
color and ∼ 0.11 M⊙ in mass. " They estimate the Teff range to be
6450K to 6650K (from most recent PM2013 updated Teff/SpT values, this
corresponds to ~F5.5V to ~F4V, and their central Teff=6550K (~F4.75V ~
"F5-V").
